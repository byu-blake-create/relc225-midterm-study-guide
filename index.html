Unit 1: Understanding Latter-day Saint Doctrine and History

  1. Who is the chief cornerstone of the restored Church?
    Jesus Christ.
  2. Why and how is Jesus Christ considered the chief cornerstone of the Church?
    He is the foundation of salvation, revelation, ordinances, covenant identity, and Church authority; everything in the Church is meant to point to and depend on Him.
  3. What is the fundamental doctrine of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints?
    The fundamental doctrine is the Atonement and Resurrection of Jesus Christ, with all other doctrines as appendages to that central truth.
  4. How are Latter-day Saints unique in their beliefs about Christ when compared with other Christians?
    Latter-day Saints teach that the Father and Son are distinct divine beings, Christ has a glorified resurrected body, priesthood authority was restored, and modern revelation continues through prophets.
  5. What are the six foundational time periods of Church history we cover in this class (titles and years)?
    1) Beginnings (1820-1831), 2) Two Church Centers (1831-1838), 3) Nauvoo (1838-1846), 4) Church in the West (1846-1950), 5) Expansion (1950-1989), 6) Worldwide Church (1989-present).
  6. Where was the Church headquartered in each major period?
    Beginnings: New York (Palmyra/Manchester/Fayette); Two Church Centers: Kirtland, Ohio and Missouri centers (Independence/Far West); Nauvoo period: Nauvoo, Illinois; Church in the West, Expansion, and Worldwide periods: Salt Lake City/Utah as administrative center.
  7. Why was a second Church center established in Independence, Missouri?
    By revelation, Independence was designated as the place for Zion (the New Jerusalem) and a temple center.
  8. According to the Doctrine and Covenants, how are we to "seek learning"?
    By study and also by faith (D&C 88:118).
  9. What databases and online resources are available to aid in searching Church doctrine and history?
    Key resources include Gospel Library, Joseph Smith Papers, Church History Topics, BYU Religious Studies Center, scriptures.byu.edu, and General Conference corpus/search tools.
  10. What is "doctrine"?
    Doctrine is divine, saving truth taught by the Lord through scripture and prophetic authority; not every statement, policy, or practice carries the same doctrinal weight.
  11. How do principles, ordinances, covenants, policies, programs, and practices relate to doctrine?
    Doctrine gives eternal truth; principles guide application; ordinances and covenants ritualize and bind; policies/programs/practices are inspired administrative applications that may adapt over time.
  12. What are the four levels of evaluating an official Latter-day Saint teaching?
    1) Canonized scripture, 2) united statements of the First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve, 3) repeated teachings of general authorities over time, 4) official Church publications/instructional materials.
  13. What are five factors for evaluating a historical source?
    Authorship/provenance, time gap from event, purpose/audience and bias, corroboration with other sources, and evidentiary support/context.

Unit 2: The First Vision

  1. What is the Great Apostasy?
    A post-apostolic loss of priesthood authority and corruption/loss of key doctrines and ordinances after the deaths of the original Apostles.
  2. What is the Protestant Reformation?
    A movement (1500s onward) challenging medieval Catholic authority and doctrine, promoting scriptural access and reform.
  3. What is American religious freedom?
    Constitutional protection (especially First Amendment) for free exercise of religion and disestablishment of a national church.
  4. What is the Second Great Awakening?
    An early-19th-century Protestant revival movement in the U.S., marked by camp meetings, conversion emphasis, and intense denominational growth.
  5. What is the Burned-over District?
    A term for parts of upstate New York heavily affected by repeated revival preaching and religious competition.
  6. Why are there different First Vision accounts?
    Different audiences, purposes, contexts, and remembered emphases produced different details while preserving the same core event.
  7. How many primary First Vision accounts did Joseph write, and when?
    Four firsthand accounts are usually identified: 1832, 1835, 1838, and 1842. Joseph either wrote them himself or had scribes record them under his direction.
  8. What was the audience/context for each of Joseph's accounts?
    1832: personal history narrative (focus on forgiveness); 1835: conversation record with a visitor; 1838: official Church history for Saints/public record; 1842: summary for non-LDS audience (Wentworth context).
  9. Who wrote secondary First Vision accounts, and what unique information do they provide?
    Examples include Orson Pratt, Orson Hyde, Levi Richards, David Nye White, and Alexander Neibaur; they preserve remembered details such as wording about light, opposition, two personages, and Joseph's reporting to others.
  10. What are two primary doctrinal messages from the First Vision?
    The Father and the Son are distinct divine beings, and revelation/prophetic calling was restored in modern times.
  11. What is a creed?
    A formal statement of belief that defines doctrinal boundaries for a faith community.
  12. What are the basic Christian creeds?
    The Apostles' Creed, Nicene Creed, and Athanasian Creed are standard historic examples (with Chalcedonian definitions often included in broader creedal orthodoxy).
  13. How do creeds define a modern "Christian"?
    In many traditions, a Christian is one who affirms creedal teachings about God, Christ, and salvation, especially Trinitarian formulations.
  14. What does it mean that churches "deny [God's] power"?
    In Restoration teaching, it means preserving outward forms of religion while lacking divine authority, priesthood ordinances, and the fulness of revealed power.

Unit 3: The Book of Mormon

  1. When and how did Joseph obtain his brown seer stone, and what did he use it for?
    Around 1822, Joseph found a brown seer stone while helping dig a well; he used it as an instrument for receiving revelation, including in Book of Mormon translation work.
  2. How many years passed from Moroni's first visit until Joseph obtained the plates?
    Four years (1823 to 1827).
  3. What did Joseph need to repent of before obtaining the plates?
    He had to repent of pride and worldly motives, including seeking gain or status rather than God's purposes.
  4. What year did Joseph obtain the gold plates?
    1827 (September 22).
  5. What is the definition of "Urim and Thummim"?
    A scriptural term for sacred revelatory instruments used to receive divine knowledge; early Saints sometimes used the term broadly for both the Nephite interpreters and Joseph Smith's seer stone.
  6. How was the Book of Mormon translated?
    By the gift and power of God through revelatory means, not conventional linguistic translation.
  7. What instruments were used in Book of Mormon translation?
    Nephite interpreters (often called Urim and Thummim) and a seer stone.
  8. What is the scriptural definition of "the gospel"?
    Faith in Jesus Christ, repentance, baptism, receiving the Holy Ghost, and enduring to the end through Christ's Atonement.
  9. How is the Book of Mormon the "most correct" book?
    It teaches the plain and precious doctrine of Christ with covenant clarity; living its teachings brings a person closer to God.
  10. What are at least four unique yet foundational Latter-day Saint teachings in the Book of Mormon?
    Examples: the doctrine of Christ (faith, repentance, baptism, Holy Ghost, enduring to the end); covenant scattering and gathering of Israel; Christ's post-Resurrection ministry in the Americas; and plain teaching about revelation, priesthood authority, and sacred ordinances.
  11. Who were the Three Witnesses of the Book of Mormon plates?
    Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris.

Unit 4: Restoration of the Priesthood and Founding of the Church

  1. Difference between priesthood power, authority, offices, keys, and ordinances?
    Power: God's power. Authority: right to act in God's name. Offices: assigned roles in priesthood quorums. Keys: governing right to direct priesthood use. Ordinances: authorized sacred acts performed under keys.
  2. How did Joseph Smith receive the priesthood?
    By angelic ordination: John the Baptist restored Aaronic Priesthood; Peter, James, and John restored Melchizedek Priesthood and apostolic authority.
  3. What priesthood and keys did John the Baptist restore?
    Aaronic Priesthood with keys of ministering of angels, gospel of repentance, and baptism by immersion for remission of sins.
  4. What priesthood and keys did Peter, James, and John restore?
    Melchizedek Priesthood, including apostolic authority and keys of the kingdom.
  5. What occurred in the "chamber of father Whitmer," and relation to office of elder?
    In the Whitmer home (Fayette area), key revelations and ordinations were given as Church organization approached; the office of elder functioned as the leading priesthood office in the Church's initial organization.
  6. What occurred at the June 1831 conference regarding "high priesthood" / Melchizedek Priesthood?
    High priests were ordained in conference, marking expanded organization and functioning of Melchizedek Priesthood offices.
  7. What other angels are part of the Melchizedek Priesthood restoration?
    In broader restoration sequence, Moses, Elias, and Elijah restored additional priesthood keys in the Kirtland Temple (1836).
  8. Which priesthood offices existed at Church organization, and which developed later?
    Initially active: deacon, teacher, priest, elder. Later developed/organized over time: bishop, high priest, patriarch, apostles, seventies, and fuller quorum structures.
  9. How did Church organization develop over time?
    It unfolded line upon line by revelation: from a small covenant community into quorums, stakes, councils, auxiliaries, and global administration.
  10. Where are the "Articles and Covenants" found in D&C, and how were they created?
    Doctrine and Covenants 20. They were compiled by inspiration from prior revelations, scriptural language, and revealed instruction for organizing the Church.
  11. How, when, and where was the Church organized?
    April 6, 1830, in Fayette, New York, by prior revelation and legal/religious organization with ordained officers and sustaining vote.
  12. Four foundational Church events listed in D&C 20?
    Joseph Smith's divine calling and manifestation from God, receiving power from on high, the coming forth/translation of the Book of Mormon, and the formal organization of the Church.
  13. Six foundational Church doctrines listed in D&C 20?
    God's reality and creation, the Fall, Atonement of Christ, faith/repentance, baptism and Holy Ghost, and enduring covenant discipleship.
  14. Foundational Church practices listed in D&C 20?
    Baptism, confirmation, sacrament administration, ordination, record-keeping, regular meetings, teaching, prayer, and discipline/accountability.
  15. Five titles Joseph Smith was sustained as at organization?
    Seer, translator, prophet, apostle of Jesus Christ, and elder of the Church.

Unit 5: History and Purpose of the Doctrine and Covenants

  1. What are the Manuscript Revelation Books?
    Early handwritten compilation volumes preserving revealed texts before formal publication; foundational sources behind printed revelations.
  2. What is the 1833 Book of Commandments?
    The first attempt to publish Joseph Smith's revelations in book form, printed in Missouri before press destruction interrupted full distribution.
  3. What type of documents are in the Doctrine and Covenants?
    Mostly modern revelations, inspired declarations, letters/instructions, and official statements addressed to individuals, councils, and the Church.
  4. What were the original "doctrine" and "covenants" parts that created the title?
    "Doctrine" referred to the Lectures on Faith; "Covenants and Commandments" referred to revealed sections (later called Doctrine and Covenants).
  5. Major D&C editions and significance?
    1833 Book of Commandments (first publication attempt), 1835 Doctrine and Covenants (adds lectures and many revelations), 1844/1876/1921/1981 revisions (reordering, additions, headings), and later updates including Official Declarations.
  6. Which revelation was given as a "preface" for the Doctrine and Covenants?
    Doctrine and Covenants section 1.
  7. Key principles when considering D&C revelations (including why wording changed)?
    Revelation is given in historical context, often in living language; editorial refinements clarified grammar or audience while preserving revealed intent and doctrine.
  8. What is "canon"?
    Officially accepted scripture binding for the Church.
  9. What are the Stewardship, Brethren, and Front Door principles for personal revelation?
    Stewardship: revelation comes within your responsibility. Brethren: true revelation aligns with united prophetic leadership. Front Door: new doctrine/order enters through the Lord's appointed prophet and Church channels, not private claims.
  10. What book of scripture is the "foundation" of the Church?
    Jesus Christ is the chief cornerstone of the Church. In Restoration teaching, the Book of Mormon is commonly called the keystone of our religion and a foundational witness of the Restoration.
  11. What is "Joseph's Promise" regarding the Doctrine and Covenants?
    There is not a standard canonized phrase by that exact title; the safest idea is that Joseph testified these revelations came from God, and the Doctrine and Covenants promises blessings to those who hear and heed the Lord's voice.

Unit 6: Additional Restoration Scripture

  1. What does it mean that Joseph Smith "translated" the Bible?
    He made an inspired revision (JST), restoring clarity and lost meaning rather than translating from ancient manuscripts in a standard scholarly method.
  2. How was Joseph's Bible translation accomplished?
    By revelation through dictation and revision with scribes, working from an English Bible text and inspired correction/expansion.
  3. By what names has Joseph's Bible translation been known?
    Joseph Smith Translation (JST), Inspired Version, and New Translation of the Bible.
  4. What types of changes did Joseph Smith make to the Bible?
    Doctrinal clarifications, narrative additions, inspired restorations, harmonizations, and language refinements.
  5. How does JST contribute to restoration of truth and Joseph's education?
    It restored key doctrines and became a revelatory schooling process for Joseph, often prompting new revelations that became D&C sections.
  6. How many changes did Joseph make to the Bible, and how many are in the LDS Bible today?
    About 3,400 inspired revisions are commonly cited. Only a selection appears in the Latter-day Saint Bible footnotes, appendix, and study helps; many more are preserved in full JST/Inspired Version materials.
  7. What was the process of acquiring and printing the JST?
    Joseph dictated revisions to scribes (1830s); manuscripts stayed with Emma's line and were later published by the RLDS tradition; LDS access expanded over time and selected JST material was added to LDS Bible helps.
  8. Why was Joseph's Bible translation considered open-ended?
    Because revelation is continuing; Joseph did not frame the work as permanently closed from future light and truth.
  9. Where did the Book of Abraham come from?
    From Egyptian papyri obtained in Kirtland in 1835, with text produced by revelation through Joseph Smith.
  10. When was the Book of Abraham translated?
    Primarily beginning in 1835, with publication in 1842.
  11. What are some academic challenges and responses regarding the Book of Abraham?
    Challenges include papyri dating and surviving fragments not matching the full text directly; faithful responses include missing papyrus theory, catalyst theory, and emphasis on revelatory translation rather than strict one-to-one linguistic rendering.
  12. What does it mean that Abraham desired the "blessings of the fathers"?
    He sought priesthood covenant blessings, righteous posterity, and participation in the Abrahamic covenant promises.
  13. What is the Abrahamic covenant?
    God's covenant promising Abraham priesthood blessings, posterity, land/inheritance, and that all nations would be blessed through his seed and the Messiah.
  14. Who may the planet Kolob best symbolize?
    In Abraham 3, Kolob is presented as the great governing star nearest the throne of God and is used to teach order, rank, reckoning of time, and divine governance.
  15. What are the facsimiles in the Pearl of Great Price, and what can they represent?
    They are the three published illustrations that accompany the Book of Abraham (Facsimiles 1, 2, and 3), presented with Joseph Smith's explanations and used in Latter-day Saint study alongside the Abraham text.
  16. What main gospel themes are present in the Book of Abraham?
    Premortal life, council in heaven, divine identity and potential, priesthood covenant, creation by divine planning, and mortal testing.
  17. How did we get the Pearl of Great Price, and when was it canonized?
    Compiled from Joseph's inspired materials (Book of Moses, Abraham, Joseph Smith-Matthew, Joseph Smith-History, Articles of Faith), first published in 1851 and canonized by the Church in 1880.

Unit 7: Zion, Gathering of Israel, and the Law of Consecration

  1. What is the Lamanite Mission and its major effect on the Church?
    The 1830-31 mission to Native peoples west of Missouri also led missionaries through Ohio, where many (including Sidney Rigdon's congregations) converted, rapidly expanding the Church.
  2. Where was the Church first commanded to gather in this dispensation?
    Early gathering instructions centered first on Ohio (Kirtland), then additionally on Missouri as Zion's center place.
  3. What is the City of Zion, or the New Jerusalem?
    A covenant community and prophesied holy city of Saints built on celestial law, justice, unity, and temple worship.
  4. Where will the latter-day city of Zion be built?
    In Independence, Jackson County, Missouri (the center place identified by revelation).
  5. What was Joseph Smith's vision for Zion?
    A holy, ordered, economically just society centered on temples, covenant discipleship, and care for the poor under Christ's law.
  6. What are some definitions for the word Zion?
    A people of one heart and one mind; the pure in heart; covenant stakes of safety; the New Jerusalem; and the cause of building God's kingdom.
  7. Which D&C section is known as "the Law" of the Church?
    Doctrine and Covenants section 42.
  8. What types of laws are included in "the Law"?
    Moral law, missionary and teaching responsibilities, consecration/stewardship principles, care for poor and sick, chastity/family standards, and Church governance instructions.
  9. Definitions/differences between "all things common," United Firm (Order), and law of consecration?
    "All things common" is broad communal sharing language; the United Firm/Order was a specific administrative stewardship arrangement among selected leaders/business properties; the law of consecration is the higher covenant principle of dedicating all to God through stewardship, agency, and accountability.
  10. What are celestial principles of consecration?
    Stewardship not ownership, willing sacrifice, unity, equality with accountability, labor and self-reliance, care for poor and needy, and placing all resources/time/talents in the service of God.
